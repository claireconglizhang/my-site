Coming from an educator, teacher, and school administrator background, I am passionate about education systems change. I am dedicated to my role as an applied researcher who bridges the gaps between scientific research and educational policy and practice and utilizes the processes and data that lead to the positive outcomes for teachers and students.

I hold a Ph.D. in Quantitative Research Methods in Education with a specialization in Educational Data Science from University of Oregon and a master’s degree in Organizational Leadership from Northeastern University. Currently I am a senior statistician at San Francisco Unified School District and the lead of collecting, managing, analyzing, and reporting English Learners data. I also contribute to disseminating and presenting analysis results to inform decision-makings on English Learner program placement, implementation, and evaluation throughout the school district.

I have kept honing my research skills by applying my educational data science expertise to a variety of research projects. My research competencies include but are not limited to experimental and quasi-experimental research design, generalized linear models, multilevel modeling, structural equation modeling, advanced measurement and assessment methods, program evaluation, policy analysis, implementation science, meta-analysis, scholarly writing, and grant writing. My research centers on quasi-experimental studies of school-, teacher-, family-, and student-level factors on student performance as well as non-academic outcomes in K-12 education. My dissertation consists of two separate, quasi-experimental studies investigating the impacts of teacher human capital characteristics, advisor-advisee relationships, and classroom teacher-student relationships on student performance and attitude. Both independently and in collaboration with my co-authors, I have led several research projects and presented my papers in multiple national conferences such as AERA, AEFP, and SREE.
